Introduction

As an AI developer, you know that finding the right information quickly is crucial for your applications. However, traditional keyword-based search methods often fall short in complex scenarios, leading to irrelevant or missing results. This pain point is all too familiar: users complain about searches that miss obvious matches while surfacing irrelevant documents.

In this article, we'll explore the differences between semantic search and keyword search, when to use each approach, and how MrMemory can help you build a more effective AI agent memory.

What is Semantic Search?

Semantic search is an AI-powered technique that understands meaning and intent behind your query instead of just matching keywords. This approach transforms your text into vector embeddings, which capture the meaning and context of the content. By comparing these vectors, semantic search systems can find conceptually related content even when queries and documents share no common words.

How Does Semantic Search Work?

Semantic search combines several AI technologies to analyze relationships between words, understand context, and interpret what you're actually trying to find. The process involves three key stages:

  1. Neural network generation: Transformer models like BERT create embeddings through specialized architectures.
  2. Similarity calculations: Cosine similarity compares vectors to measure semantic closeness.
  3. Relevance ranking: Rank results based on their semantic relevance.

When to Use Semantic Search

Semantic search is particularly useful in complex, conversational, or ambiguous query scenarios where keyword systems fall short. This approach is ideal for applications that require a deep understanding of the user's intent and context.

What is Keyword Search?

Keyword search, on the other hand, is a traditional method that matches exact words or phrases. This approach is suitable for simple queries where the user's intent is clear and well-defined.

When to Use Keyword Search

Keyword search is ideal for applications that require exact matching, such as searching for specific product codes or database records.
